Chapter 3


1

“Because it’s a funeral, Romin,” I said.

“No, but seriously,” he said. “Sylvia might be tall, but Valentina’s that perfect kind of short, you know?”

“A funeral,” I repeated, covering Ellie’s ears. She sat in my lap, kicking her legs against the bottom of the chair as attendees filled the auditorium. “And—you’re wrong.”

“No way. A girl like Valentina is so much more feminine.”

“You wish,” I said. “Valentina’s like five-six on a good day. Sylvia’s five-eleven on a bad day. Meaning she takes up more space. Meaning she’s more ‘woman’ than Valentina, meaning she’s more feminine. Plus, Valentina is flat.”

“But her a—” he looked at Ellie, whimsically playing with strands of her hair— “she’s thick, you know, in a good way,” Romin said, “you wish you had it like that.”

“No. Sylvia’s way more perfect all around,” I said. “Her chest is to die for. But they’re both above an eight, at least.”

Romin said, “Yeah. Maybe nine. But if Sylvia just lost a little—”

“Hey boys,” Sylvia said. She lifted Ellie out of my lap as she took the seat next to me, wrapping her arms around her like a protective belt. “What’s the gossip about me?”

“Yeah, why don’t you finish that thought, Romin?” I said.

“No, just . . . we were saying that . . .” he muttered, until Valentina came to save the conversation, bundled with her crimson scarf and a stylish hat that concealed her face.

“Your grandfather was kind of a big deal, wasn’t he,” Valentina said. “For a soldier like him, you’ve got Carmine and Merlot reaching across the aisle to honor him. And even some of the Generals from the Compass Rose, by gods…”

The auditorium was already flooded in full attendance. On the walls were silhouettes from torn-down religious sculptures: more than likely, I knew this place used to be a grand chapel, with three stories of seats fanned out over a quarter-circle. Black paint covered the gold-plated words BLESSED BE THE GODS AND THEIR PRESENCE AS GUARDIANS IN THIS WORLD, AND BLESSED BE THE CHOSEN. Two sets of marble feet stood on two different pedestals without a body to match: the words GODDESS EVELLA XCIV and CHOSEN ULLRICH XCIV hardly discernible though all the scratches.

There were so many talking voices already. Chatter was indiscernible and inseparable like a fast-running stream, and soon they were hushed whispers as the Grandmaster General approached the stage. An old, enlarged tin-type of my grandfather hung in the center, eyes posed like those portraits in museums, staring all his attendees in the face at the same time, face covered in soot, posed in the streets of an unidentifiable town, maybe taken some fifty-or-so years ago when he was my age.

“We gather here to celebrate the life of Everett Moses Berguard,” the Grandmaster said, “one of the last soldiers who fought in the Fall of Nordhaven and lived to tell the tale, one of the brave warriors who saved the lives of innocent refugees after the Chymaera destroyed the city. In his early life, Everett was born an only child to his parents Francis and Elizabeth Berguard . . .”

Only child? I thought. But Clint said his father had a brother . . .

“ . . . whose life was taken by a Chymaeran hostile within the city, hiding among us: proof that the curse is a very real threat . . .”

But it happened the night before, the night of the map, I thought. I watched Clint’s expression, but didn’t see any reaction.

“ . . . and soldiers like Everett are the reason that the King, the Queen, and the rest of the royal family were able to survive. Let them not be a footnote in the annals of history. Everett was a hero, a fighter, a legend, and the reasont that—”

The main doors suddenly flung outward somewhere on the first story, slamming against the wall, and everybody’s faces spun from the general.

“He was a coward!”

The voice echoed through the halls. A short black-haired woman emerged from the doors. “He’s the reason the Chosen was killed beyond the walls! He’s doomed our people, and killed our only chance at salvation through his cowardice!”

The challenger heaved in several short breaths while the rest of the auditorium tried to catch up, still pressing towards the stage: soft conversations in hushed voices while the General picked his next words in retaliation.

Guards approached from the sides, weapons drawn. Interruption of the Grandmaster General, especially with blasphemy, was a crime punishable by death—just speaking out of turn could get a cadet beaten.

“No, let’s hear her out,” the Grandmaster General said. He looked at her clothes: tattered and worn, no sign of any Academy mark or gear. “And what would you know about heroes, young lady?”

“I know that heroes don’t let their nieces and nephews die,” she said, “they don’t protect themselves at the cost of childrens’ lives. Especially the child who was slated to become the next Chosen: the child of the purest blood, the son of Ullrich.”

The audience scoffed. Laughter filled the halls: “Why, those are children’s tales,” the Grandmaster said. “Out of sympathy for your ignorance, I will say this once: take your delusions from this place and leave at once.”

“Ullrich was a legend told to rile up cadets,” another attendee shouted.

“The blood of the Chosen lives on,” she exclaimed. “Leave no room for doubt on the existence of the Gods, I am Ullrich’s daughter!”

“Damn Merlot and your delusions,” an attendee barked. “There’s no room for fairy tales!”

“It is vested within me. It was transferred from my father. And it lives on in the ultimate sacrifice of my brother. I am the next Chosen!”

The auditorium filled with laughter and disapproval from both the Carmine and the Merlot. “Get this kid back to Seminary,” someone shouted, before the guards restrained her.

“Did you not see the light? The amulet has been uncovered,” she shouted, “meaning legitimate blood has made contact, don’t you all understand? You’ll never stop the advancement of the Absolution!”

“Alright, that’s enough,” the Grandmaster said. “May you all remember that great heroes like Everett show the magnitude of their significance in their opposition: the proof that they truly have made a difference against those Chymaeran sympathizers.” But my father’s words kept running through my mind. The letter stained with his blood rested in my pocket. It was a chance I couldn’t avoid.

“Titus! Where are you going,” Sylvia said.

“Your speech,” Valentina said, “it’s up next, isn’t it?”

The words were still in my mind, but the incongruences edged me deeper. I ascended the marble stairs of the balcony, sliding my hands against the golden handrails that were all saturated in black paint, then passing through the balcony’s entrance arch whose damaged letters were indiscernible.

I caught a glimpse of her on the first story, escorted from the building by the officers, and I felt her name rest on the tip of my tongue, waiting to be released. I flung myself through the ballroom, down the stairs, to the outside as they approached the CITY GUARD carriage, heaving breaths between my footsteps, if I could just confirm the truth.

“Lyra!” I shouted.

She turned her head, surprised to hear the sound of her own name.

“Lyra, who are you to Everett?”

“Usher her in, quick,” one of the guards said. “You! Get back inside, or leave immediately.”

“I’m his niece,” she said, “and Ullrich was my father, how do you all in this city know nothing of the real truth?”

This city? You mean . . .

“You have one chance, stranger, a choice that will decide the fate of my bloodline’s last chance at a Chosen. Fight them off with me, or forever hold your silence,” she said.

“Wait, hold on . . . “

“Last warning! Step back or you will be engaged,” the guards said.

I struggled between choices. It felt as if my soul was tearing down the middle. The disapproving glare in Lyra’s face marked me as if I was one of the ignorant, and the guards’ glare read the same.

“Last chance,” she shouted. But then all the memories of Academy life came back, the hopes of becoming a financial support for my sister, the world beyond the walls as a Dragon-class cadet—

The guards’ sight was entirely on me: and sick of waiting, Lyra found a window. She flipped her closest captor over her shoulder, unsnapping the revolver from his waist as she flicked the chamber.

Blam. Crimson gore erupted from the guard’s chest: before he could react, she primed the hammer again. Blam. Gray matter sprayed across the cobblestone walk as another guard collapsed to the ground, saturated in the horrified face of the third officer. Click. Blam. The officer was holding his chest: the wetness spread across his chest as a single streak descended his shirt, then she turned to me, and hesitated for a moment—

The gunshot erupted through the air, but I wasn’t sure who it came from, until I watched the muzzle of the stolen gun without a trail of smoke departing the barrel, and the roll of her eyes as she grabbed her chest.

There were spectators forming behind me, horrified, and the congregation watched from the windows of the dead chapel, whose spirits of past gods were exorcised with the advancement of the city’s new regime. She crumpled to the ground. Four large pools of red sifted between the cracks of cobblestone, and I dashed to lift the gut-shot guard from the ground, taking his arm upon my shoulder, administering whatever lasting remnants of Sylvia’s surgical small-talk could help me, placing pressure on the wound, shouting for medical attention, or anyone, feeling the warm blood of the victims trickle down my face.

Vera’s life ended as loudly as her first introduction, and I thought I would never speak to her again. Or not in this life, at least.



2

That same scene repeated over and over again in my mind: branded in my synapses like an iron marked livestock. The Academy trained us for this. They told us people would die, to prepare ourselves for separation, that attachment was the greatest enemy, although brotherhood was one of their core values.

But not here. It was supposed to happen far beyond the walls. And not now. It was supposed to happen years from now.

“You have to talk to me, Titus,” the therapist said.

“I’m fine, really,” I said. “Just clear me for training. We’re all gonna see this at some point, I guess I was just a little early.”

“You were held at gunpoint,” he said, eyes narrowing behind his bifocals as he leaned back in his chair. “You didn’t just watch people die, you were almost one of them.”

“I know, I know,” I said, “my priority was just saving the wounded officer.”

“And you are a hero,” he said.

Their faces still move in my mind. Their expressions still consume me. That thousand-yard-stare at the edge of death when you know you’re going to die, and your mind races to every memory and experience just to try to understand what it’s about to go through, just watching that in…

“It’s in your eyes, Titus,” he said. “The same look the surviving soldiers wear when they return from beyond the walls, so I know you’re not clear for combat or anything, for that matter.”

“Then what? Some meds? A hot bath? What do I need to prove I’m fine?”

That last look of disappointment in Lyra’s eyes before she collapsed, the true color of gray matter was so different than I expected. . .

“At least three weeks,” the therapist said, “and then we’ll consider it. Your heroics are the reason why we’re giving you the time off, one week, so spend it to recover, relax, and replenish the emotional fuel you lost.”

He handed me a bottle of orange, oval-shaped pills.

“And don’t drink on those,” he said. Good. Well, I’m nothing like my father, anyway. I snatched the medication from the desk. They rattled in my pocket as I left. I felt my fingers check my face after a certain warmth out of habit, but there was nothing there, no clumps of others’ flesh and blood, just smooth skin.


3

The vertical trolley descended from the station: the last ride of the day, and my bags were packed beside me. There wasn’t much to pack, after all, it was as if half my life was in Auntie Elma’s house—and the GREETINGS, CADET brochure still rested in my apartment on the desk.

“All aboard,” the attendant called. The steam engine grunted in response. It was just me and him on the second story, and a full panorama of Blackwater, so vast and expansive you couldn’t see walls on the horizon. And for an entire life within the walls, it often felt like they were never there.

You could tell the city districts from the color of the lights: the first tier at the foothills flooded with incandescents, so bright the grass on the hills looked like it was daytime. Children played in the lawns on the sides of the mountains while mothers and fathers stoked fire pits, wearing nothing but short sleeves and shorts. The second tier was halfway to the wall of blizzarding conditions: mixed incandescents and flickering flames in the windowsills, crunched complexes where many lived close together. Then the third died out in weak, distant flames, saturated in snow, thatch metal roofing saturated with a foot of fresh powder. The fourth district wasn’t even a district: it was completely dark.

“You’re out here awful late,” a voice squeaked behind me.

“And I thought you couldn’t leave Intelligence,” I said, “so what are you doing here, Isla?” She was so short I must have missed her—hair she dyed silver that reflected in the moonlight.

“Intelligence, actually,” she said. “A formidable foe beyond our walls. I’m on a research trip. Have you heard of the activity?”

“Not anything new, though I was just out there,” I said, summarizing our adventure beyond the walls. “So long as you don’t tell anyone. Or else I’ll have to kill you.”

She laughed, but as soon as our eyes made contact, she turned away. She inched closer to me, her head almost at chest-level, pursing her lips as she searched for another conversation starter.

Cobblestone. Blood. Flesh. The images burned a hole through my heart like acid, leaving a certain emptiness within me. When I felt her soft skin graze me, I felt a spark of something I had been missing for a long time, something that eluded me, something that I patiently deferred for the longest time.

“But at least I’m off for the night,” she said. “I heard about your heroics the other day, helping save an officer’s life.”

I remembered my cowardice in hesitation. I couldn’t shake it. “Yeah, I just did what I could,” I said, feeling some force draw me closer to her, just to feel our skin touch again.

“What are you doing tonight? Quite the coincidence I ran into you here,” said Isla. Her Dragon mark fluttered behind her back. “Most of the time I’m seeing you in matches.”

“Yeah, and most of the time, it’s us looking up at you,” I said.

She returned another unrequited laugh. “Well, it’s impressive enough for both of us to be at the top.”

“Sure is,” I said, and I could clearly see the end of this conversation—we were a little over halfway to the bottom. Sylvia’s eyes flashed in my mind, the feeling of her skin, the softness of her, a feeling of regret for something I hadn’t done yet, trying to calculate the weight of my decision.

“What are you doing tonight?” Isla asked.

I paused for a minute, watching clouds of my breath dissolve into the air—as we descended from the Academy training grounds in the Compass Rose, the temperature plummeted, until snowflakes appeared in front of us. The warmth of her skin felt so nice in contrast, and I couldn’t resist thinking of what was beyond Sylvia’s barriers, as much as I hated it, it had been so long, and with all the pain, I just . . .

I said, “I gotta take care of my sister.”

She looked down at her feet. “Oh, that’s too bad,” Isla said. “Maybe next time we can grab a drink?”

Her skin looked so soft, and . . . “Sure,” I said, “sounds great. Well, I guess you know where to find me.”

“At your old house, or studying the rest of the time in the Grand Library. But reading more books isn’t gonna make beating us any easier, Tie,” Isla replied.

I felt the tram car click at the bottom of the rails. She waved good-bye to me, and I waved back, a strange sense of integrity mixing with the hollowness of the faces and the exhaustion of the loneliness as I set out for my sister.



3

I tucked her in bed with her stuffed wolf, the same one that used to be mine a long time ago. I was originally the one that named it Wolfie, and she was so excited when I handed it down to her that she felt like she just had to keep its name the same.

I watched her as she turned in bed, adjusting to the mattress and sheets like a dog or cat. The Owl, the Cardinal, and all the other birds were off getting drunk somewhere—neither even bothered to leave Ellie any food or water, so she was starving when I got back, saying she was gonna take some money from Auntie’s purse and head down to the corner store (though I caught her just in time, she was probably bluffing.)

I hear her whisper to me: “Hey, Tie.”

“Yeah, little bug?”

“There were people at the door today,” she said.

“When?”

“After Auntie left, but I didn’t answer cause they were strangers.”

I asked her what they looked like. “Hats, but it was dark so I couldn’t see because I was hiding,” she said.

“That’s good,” I said—probably the postman, since we’re among the furthest houses in the district. “You get to sleep, now, so you can wake up for Seminary tomorrow, okay?”

“Okay, Tie,” she said.

“I love you, Ellie,” I whispered, hoping for a certain answer, but—

“I know,” she replied.

I lightly closed the door of her room after shutting the window, the last crack of electric light sputtering through the crack, reflecting off her flaxen hair until the strip vanished and her gentle breaths filled the room. My childhood bedroom was down the hall: door still open, a single candle illuminating the room in flickering light (because we couldn’t afford electricity everywhere), though the darkness was deceiving. Auntie Cardinal turned it into a closet. Boxes piled all across the floor, filled with her sewing works and anything else she could jam in there, but the boxes of wool were soft enough that I could make a bed, so long as Auntie didn’t catch me in the morning, or else she’d spill some insulting complaint on why my father just had to adopt me, since zero was enough in the first place, and I’d just smile and nod or apologize profusely.

One of the books from the expedition was turned over like a hardcover bridge between the boxes, and I lifted it: The Phantom Absolution, a black-text fiction piece on heroes journeying beyond the wall, the same book that Sylvia was reading earlier. A group of heroes from different races: the Ahkvasans, cat-people controlling the speed of time. Some race that controlled lightning. Another that controlled death. The Humans. A race of winged angels in floating lands, controlling the power of the mind, and plant-beings, and all these other creatures, so interesting, though all I could distinguish was the pictures since I couldn’t read the language.

The hand-drawn images engrossed me until I heard a knock at the door, and my thoughts instantly raced to the men that Ellie had been talking about earlier, and they sounded louder and louder, so in fear of waking Ellie, I ran down the stairs to check: quick, light footsteps as I placed my weight against the railing, allowing my footsteps to depress into the carpet.

They hadn’t heard me. The knocking continued. I looked through the eye-hole, though through the darkness, the figure was indiscernible. I slowly peered though the window, my heart racing, seeing nothing but the pitch darkness of light until a sudden flash of red terrified me, and I collapsed backwards before I could distinguish what it was.

Several breaths later, I returned to the door (quietly (enough, I think)), twisting the handle and the lock. “You scared me,” I whispered. “But what brings you here so late?”

“Another episode right now.” I could see the tears streaming from her crimson eyes, though she tried to stop them. “I just need a friend right now. And I know—I know what happened the other night, I just need you, or Romin, or Valentina, but I really need you right now,” she muttered, as her words tapered off into ramblings, though the emotion behind them was all I could hear. We could empathize with each other; two survivors cut from the same cloth that patched each other up, two people that could genuinely understand the same type of pain. So I led her by hand to the couch in the family room, cluttered with boxes, just like every other room in this house that the Cardinal had disgraced.

“It’s just, I—”

“I know, I know,” I said. “Is it alright if I touch you?”

“Please touch me,” she said, between sobs.

My arms extended around her. My left hand found its way behind her head, and my right hand embraced her side as I traced my fingers though her hair, whispering with a calming shhhhhh intonation like the sound of water, not keeping her from crying, but just being present enough for her to feel it out with someone.

It’s like I just can’t feel, she’d always explain. Like I can’t let it out and I’m just here, she’d say, and sometimes I feel like I just want to die, I just can’t feel it, and then I wonder if I’ve always been this way, if I’ve ever felt it, and then I embrace her, because no series of words ever spoke so accurately for my own pain, though instead, I so often turned it internally.

I felt the release of my own pain within me, the imagination of its release reflected in watching her, and I imagined the roles had been reversed, what it would feel like, though all I felt was this deep-seated need to protect her. “I’m not going anywhere,” I said, “and I’ll stay as long as you need me to, until you feel better.”

“But will I?” she asked, “I just . . .”

“You’ve gotten better every time before,” I said, “and you’re gonna be fine, remember what I said.”

“You win the game of life by deciding to play,” she said.

“By getting up in the morning. By breathing. For people like you and me, that’s all we have to do,” I said, “that’s all it takes, Sylvia, it’s not about money or happiness, but it’s about being content and present.”

“I know, but it just . . . I feel so disconnected,” she said. “I’m messed up, I’m broken, I’m just—”

Damaged goods. “You’re not,” I said, stopping her. “You’re mine, and I’m yours. People like us just have to look out for each other, that’s the only way we can survive.”

“I know, it’s just last night, with you, and, I just . . . W-when you left me like that, that look in your eyes, I . . . I’m sorry if it wasn’t good enough for you, I’m just not good enough, I’m sorry, I . . .” She trailed off, her sobs turning to heaving cries as I traced my nails against her back, tightening my grip against her as she melted into me.

“There you go, there you go, Sylvia. I’m not going anywhere, I’m right here,” I said, as the snow pattered against the windows, as the moonlight engulfed the two of us, my head resting above her as her sobs faded out, growing heavier and heavier with the weight of exhaustion, waiting for hours as she faded out onto the edge of sleep, and I wrapped her in a blanket, resting her head against a pillow, tracing the hair from her face and planting a kiss on her cheek. The voices and thoughts didn’t catch me: I was protecting her, after all. All she needed was some sleep and some touch. Maybe in the morning she would feel better—it was always a game of probability with this curse: both for me, and for her.

“Uh…I…” she muttered in her sleep. “Wasguna jump’f the roof,” she said, “but’came here firs, to you,” her face smothered against the couch, but I didn’t think much of it, since exhaustion had consumed me after watching both girls fall asleep.

I passed Ellie’s room: catching a glance at Wolfie on the dresser, a slight breeze catching the drapes in her room though my tired eyes dragged me down the hall like a ring from my nose. I collapsed on the clouds of wool, blissfully unaware of anything beyond the onset of sleep as my eyes weighed shut, dreaming of water and glowing lights, just as Sylvia had recurring dreams of fire.
