“Hey, son.”

“Hey, Clint,” I said.

He motioned to come in from the cold and the temperature climbed a near thirty degrees in transition. Sweat soaked the front of his thin, white shirt in an upside-down triangle, despite where the dirt from his face wiped across the fabric, and it almost felt like an Academy gym.

The smell of stuffiness made me wonder which habit of his had caused it—depended on whether the back door was locked or unlocked, but the bruise on his neck made me hope I wasn’t getting another sister (but the shop looked a lot cleaner since I left it, so maybe it was a mix of both.)

“So what’dya find out there? Must’a gone further since you had your pals,” he said, wiping the sweat from his forehead and his jet-black beard.

“Trinkets, odds, and ends,” I said. “But there’s one thing.”

“What’s that?”

The metallic box crumpled in Romin’s impatience, but the artifact still survived. “Some kind of necklace,” I said, “has some strange glow to it. If it’s essence, we’ll have to sell it underground like black-text literature. What’s your take on it?”

“Huh,” he said, drawing glasses from his pocket, peering through the single lens that still held glass. “You know the Carmine?”

“Yeah, Romin’s folk,” I said.

“Well, they wear necklaces just like this, get ‘em when you cross the initiation. Your grandpa Everett was one of them.”

“Alright,” I said, “so why’s it important?”

Clint said, “It’s not. Probably a million of these around the capital, worn by every one of their members. Though it’s strange to find one infused with essence, must have been a really old one. Where did you find this?”

“One of the X’es on the map. Wasn’t your typical loot-house. We found a corpse in there: no Chymaeran activity, so it must have been the Humans,” I said.

Clint put an end of his glasses between his front teeth, with the same scrunch of stoicism I carried in my expression. “Give me the box,” he said, taking it to the table, setting it beneath the lanterns.

His stoicism quickly changed to a glimpse of recognition. “By Gods, wait, where was this, son?”

“One of the X’es.”

“No, but where on the map?”

I pulled it out and spread it across the table. “Right here. Surrounded by all the other X’es.”

“This map’s helped us with the shop, but you know it was never meant for that, son,” Clint said, “you remember how your grandfather lost his mind?”

“Yeah,” I said. “So what?”

“So he was searching for something. Bastard had so much guilt he never let me inside himself, but I could always feel it. He got so paranoid and intense after the Curse that he’d write on anything he could find. Tryna access something in his gone-bad mind like tryna squeeze a full glass of water out of a damp towel.”

“Then you think we found it?” I asked. He reached inside the box for the crumpled scraps.

“Hell if I know, but if you— Ah!” he exclaimed.

“What?”

A line of crimson appeared on his finger, staining the jagged, pearlescent metal, and he cursed the names of the Gods. “Khiras and Evella, your idiot friend couldn’t wait to open it ‘till he got here? Is he stupid in the head or something?”

“Gods, he’s not an idiot,” I said, “you just have to be more careful.”

“Like hell I do, son, if you could just control your retarded friends and—”

“Oh, fuck off,” I said, watching a drip of his blood travel down the necklace. “At least I can control Ellie. My sister. Your daughter. Remember?”

“You fuck off, get the fuck out of here. Sorry, who makes the money around here? Does you swingin’ a sword around like a faggot and writing flowered essays at the Academy make things best for us?”

“At least it makes our family something, something rather than a son who takes care of his sister for her absent father, and a father who can’t get rid of his wife’s shop for the best.” When he fucks broads in the back and uses her as an excuse to keep it around, I almost said.

“You son of a bitch,” he spat.

“She wasn’t a bitch.” But you sure as hell treat her like one.

“I oughtta—” Clint said, but then his blood dripped down the silver strands and absorbed itself in the center pendant, and what happened next was entirely unexpected—we were already shouting over the table.

A sudden flash blinded our eyes: the brightest red left a neon in our retinas, and I heard the half-log benches collapse against the ground as we both fell backwards, the clank of the necklace against the tin after Clint dropped it, and the crash of an aisle of goods after he hit his head against it.

A bright beam of crimson flooded from the necklace like a pillar of fire, crashing against the pointed ceiling like a reverse waterfall, filling its volume like an inverted lake, and I could hardly see it since my eyes were blinded (and I guessed that Clint’s were too), and all we could do was watch the spectacle as the glow radiated out the windows, shielding my eyes as a sudden surge of energy saturated in my circuits, rolling across the floor to take refuge behind an aisle of dried goods.

“Dad!” I shouted. “Dad, are you alright?”

“Son,” he said.

I crawled towards his voice. The luminous glow tapered out as if it were missing some part of its whole, and eventually I felt for the collar of his shirt.

“Da—Clint,” I said, “your head.”

He was bleeding from another slice across his forehead. I lifted him from the ground though my eyes couldn’t adjust to the darkness—he must have clipped the edge of the metal shelves.

“It’s alright, son, I’ve got bandages in the back,” he said. “And where’s that girlfriend of yours with the medical know-how?”

“Not the time for sarcasm,” I said. “How hard did you hit, er— what day is it?”

“Like I’d know that,” he said. “A workday. Just like yesterday. The day before that, the day before that—” he pointed to the calendar on the wall, at least twelve days behind on tearing off the dates.

“What’s my name?”

“You’re my son, how’d I forget that, you loon,” he said, “I named you. Titus Nathaniel Berguard.”

“And where do you live?”

“At our house, just down the street.”

“That’s Auntie’s house now. She bought it because you wanted to keep the house in our family. But close enough,” I said.

“I guess I live here then,” he laughed, dry and emptily with a sad, heaving cough to follow. “Well, you gonna lift me up, then?”

“Yeah, yeah,” I said.

“Well I won’t be living here for long,” Clint said. “May Everett rest in peace in the Reservoir, but he’s left us that warehouse of his. We sell that place, we’ll be able to keep this shop open forever.”

But he’s said that every time. It was just a “month or so” a year ago. And it was just a “year or so” two years ago. There’s an emptiness in his eyes when he looks at the picture of Moira on the wall. And how else can I tell his “habits” from earlier? Because I always find a sheet covering the portrait when he’s in the back-room with a “client,” as if her eyes could judge him from behind the frame.

I used a certain sincerity in my voice. “Dad, we can’t keep doing this, please. We have to move on,” I said.

He’s heard my reply countless times before, and I’ve heard his response that many times, too, so instead, he ducked his head. “You know, Tie, your mother…”

“Opened this shop with you, I know,” then I added, “Dad. But she wants us to succeed and move on, if we have to, whatever it takes for her children and husband to thrive.”

“She wouldn’t want us to sell this place,” he said. “I know I don’t want to, son. You know I was gonna pass this down to you, and you’d run this business hard, and hustle, and we’d run this shop together, you and me, and Ellie could help us.”

“But I’m in the Academy to finish my degree, Clint,” I said. “I’ll take care of Ellie and I’ll take care of you.”

“What, with engin-airing? There ain’t no space to build here. Least those Civils can fix up the walls to keep the Chymaerans out,” he said, “that’s where all the money is.”

“I’m just trying to do my best,” I said. My glance dipped towards my feet, toes tracing the hardwood floors I crawled on as a child, and it was hard to meet his eyes, and I could tell he was having a hard time meeting mine, too.

The snow pattered against the windows. I wondered how my sister was doing.

“And I am, too, son,” said Clint.

“But—” then instead, I nodded. There would be no persuading him. Not now, not ever.

“Why don’t you go check on Elizabeth and Auntie Elma,” he said.

“Sure thing,” I said. “Just eat, drink, and sleep enough. And not alcohol. And eat two meals a day, three if you can. At least. And . . . Dad, just . . . take care of yourself, okay?”

He grunted in either agreement, annoyance, or a little bit of both. I closed the door behind me, looking forward to another week of not seeing him again.

“I am caring myself, Tie, I am,” Ellie said. Her flaxen hair radiated from her head like strands of golden fiber, swaying with every stroke of the death-gripped purple crayon tightened in her fist.

“I just wanna make sure you’re okay, little bug,” I said.

“I’m fine,” she said.

“And are you eating enoug—”

“Fine,” she insisted.

I sat in the pint-size wooden chair of my old playroom: the words ‘TITUS NATHANIEL BERGUARD’ scrawled across it. Toys from a decade ago sprawled across the hardwood, and I leaned toward my toes as if my weight could shear the legs straight off—if I broke the chair, Elma would make me pay for it.

She had her friends in the other room for dinner like she always did, squawking on with small talk about their husbands and their problems (though the two weren’t ever mutually exclusive.) I asked her where Ellie’s dinner was when I entered the front door: “That’s your job, Titus, isn’t it?” she replied.

The only reason I stopped living at the Academy was because of a conversation I overheard between her and those old birds—as if our childhood house had become a retirement home—and it smelled like aged linen and smoke, unlike the lavender my mother wafted around the house.

“And what of your brother?” said the darkly-dressed crow with crow’s feet.

“Still stuck in that shop of his,” Elen crowed, dressed in bright red like a cardinal. “You know how I got this house in the first place, don’t you?”

I remember hiding under the windowsill after they thought I left, glancing over to catch a glimpse of the fur-coat lady that looked like an owl. “At quite a steal,” she said. “You said you drove him down a third of the price?”

“And I would have gone further, if he hadn’t started crying,” the Cardinal said. “The only condition was I keep the house in the family, and take care of his daughter.”

“Bless his heart,” the Owl said.

“And that girl,” the Cardinal squawked, “she’s quite the handful. Feed her two meals a day and tell her she’s pretty every so often, and that’ll keep her quiet.”

They screeched in laughter. “Haaw! Haaw! Elen, you’re awful,” they said.

So I came back every day-or-so to keep her to her word.

Ellie was quite the artist—more drawings hung in her room than Sylvia, and you could tell purple was her favorite color as she etched on with a new picture. “What’s that?” I asked.

“My friend,” she said, “and that’s Wolfie,” her stuffed animal. “He moves sometimes when I sleep.”

“What do you mean?”

“Somes times I’ll find him up on the dress after he sleeps with me,” she said, probably meaning to say dresser as she always called it.

“And Auntie doesn’t put him there?”

“No, Tie,” she said. “She doesn’t come in. Or read anymore.”

“Well, I’ll read to you,” I said. “But first, you’ve got to stop drawing and do your homework.”

“No, I’m too tired,” she said.

“Well, you have Seminary in the morning, so it needs to be done,” I replied.

I went in her backpack: the planner I bought for her had several unchecked boxes, math worksheets scrunched and stuffed in there like packing paper, and an unfinished paragraph for writing with only three words. “Ellie, this is due tomorrow.”

“I know, but I don’t want to!” she said.

“If you did your first math sheet yesterday, you wouldn’t have had to do them all tonight,” I said. “Come on.”

I snatched the drawing and she stamped her feet in protest. “But Auntie Elen never help me last night! She promise.”

“You can have this back after you finish the first one.”

“No! No, no, no, no,” she said, her amethyst eyes glowing with a familiar anger.

“Yes, little bug.”

I un-crumpled her assignment and placed it on the desk, but she swatted it away. “Stop!” she shouted.

“Why don’t y’all hush up in there,” Auntie Elen shouted.

I felt the anger swell up in my chest. I let it out my nose in an exhale as I replied, “Sorry, Auntie Elen.”

“I swear,” she muttered to her friends in a hushed tone, “sometimes I just wish I could kick ‘em to the curb,” and then they cawed in laughter.

“I wish Gran-pa Ev was here, he’s nice to me,” Ellie said, and I felt my heart break a little, but I’d be her punching bag if it healed her heart a little.

I expended more of my energy expecting nothing in return. “I love you, and I’m just trying to do my best,” I said.

“I hate you. I don’t want to,” she spat, “you should do it for me again.”

“Then how about we do it together,” I said, glancing at my watch. It was already almost midnight. Every sound from the crows in the family room pushed my anger a little further.

“Okay,” she said.

It took us a mere ten minutes, and she didn’t even need my help—I found a perfect score on her last quiz when I was un-crumpling her assignments. Watching the candlelight flicker on her face reminded me of her mother, in the smallest nuances of the creases in her face, and it filled me with a certain warmth.

“Hug before I go?” I asked, then she grunted in either agreement, annoyance, or a little bit of both.

I wrapped my arms around her, relishing in her warmth, thankful for the space she took up, and I enjoyed the weight of her as I lifted her into the air, but she rested her arms at her side, without a hug in repose.

“I love you,” I said.

“I know,” she replied.



3

The exhaustion followed me home, drained from the exchanges with my father and sister, their annoyance for my necessity in their lives though I’m the glue that keeps us together. When you spend so much time doing work on others, sometimes you forget your own existence—and everything feels automatic, like losing sense of the heartbeat in your chest or the wind that pours in your lungs with every breath.

We were the top of the Academy, Dragon Class, the top five percent, and such success didn’t come without the abandonment of something from each of us. I was an orphan a long time ago. So maybe the compulsion of family ties was lost on me, maybe I never was blessed with that innate connection, that feeling that comes from the subtlety of voice and mannerisms and looks that distinguish the bonds of blood, that pride resting in the soul like the flames of logs that lean against each other.

I was in the orphanage with Sylvia, and love wasn’t unconditional, it was earned through sweat, blood, and self-defense. Kids were mean. Adults were apathetic. And the only way I earned my mother and father was through my performance in pre-Academy, the luck of essence-yielding blood, and their own needs for financial support (and, of course, the opportunity to live closer to the warmth of the Compass Rose.) Sylvia earned it through hard work, too, but her blood ties to the essence were so pure that she was an anomaly: the reason why the doctors say her eyes glow bright crimson in the dark.

I watched them dance from across the room as I entered. Sylvia sat in her favorite chair in the corner, arms resting on the angled drafting desk with a book between her hands, but with all the commotion from lower floors, she soon gave up, stuffing a thumbnail sketch between the pages.

“How’s Clint and Ellie?” she asked.

“Same as always, no different,” I said, glancing at the cover of the novel, recognizing it from our last expedition. “Any new ideas for your drawings?”

“This one’s light on scenery,” she said. “It’s unbelievable some of the descriptions that are in here, though they’re few and far between. Describes expeditions of this team of different races,” she said. “A heavy fantasy that takes place beyond Blackwater. A race of people with wings like angels that live in floating lands, cat-like people controlling the powers of time in this heavy, hot forest, called a ‘jungle.’ Entire fields of finely-ground rocks, and bodies of water so big that you can’t see the end of them.”

“I pray one day we’ll get out of here,” I said. “You and me, and Romin, and Valentina, and Ellie, too.”

“It’s too bad I’ll have to burn this before inspection,” she said—the book had black instead of red font (denoting one of the banned pieces of literature).

“Then make it live on in your drawings,” I said, “those dreams can’t die if they persist in your mind. After all, that’s something they can never take away.”

“But it’s a crying shame they take our essence, as if sterling isn’t enough for taxes,” Sylvia said. “You know, these stories tell of humans with so much essence they could conjure fire in their palms.”

“Really makes you wonder about the homeless who stop paying, trying to go off the grid,” I said, “they find their corpses crisp and ash-black on the streets when they disappear for a month or so, implying they make it long enough to not get hung for treason.”

Sylvia said, “Maybe the Gods abandoned us a long time ago. The only religion that persists here lies in wealth, in dreams of climbing the Compass Rose. They say the whole grand mountain was a dormant volcano—and there’s a cratered lake above the clouds with water so hot you could bathe in it, with minerals so enriching your skin would emerge smoother than a newborn child’s.”

“While the eternal winter curses us down here. The Vermilions are so rich they own a double mansion—and it’s only in the foothills.”

We sat at the edge of her bed, glancing out the window from the Academy penthouses. The complex sits one of one of the Cardinal Points: giant, man-made, triangular slabs of rock that protrude from the middle of the mountain like the vanes of a compass.

“Do you believe there’s a god for our people up there?” she asked, caressing her arms with the edge of her nails. “Because I’ve been having recurring dreams of fire.”

“And what happens in those dreams?” I asked.

She said, “I stand in blinding darkness, the sensation of lapping waves beneath my toes. The bright glow of crimson envelops the chamber, and all of a sudden I’m consumed in fire, in flames so hot that all the water evaporates, and shouts and screams echo from off the walls. But I’m not afraid, and it doesn’t hurt me.”

“I’ve had dreams of darkness, too,” I said, “but mine were of water, deep beneath the surface of an infinite lake, the faintest glow of something in the distance though I felt myself suffocate.”

“The books say our people were given the gift of fire and life,” Sylvia says, her radiant glance returned by my eyes as I watched her cherry-red lips, trying to remember their succulence and the softness of her skin, and I could feel the spark of us again.

I felt myself recline against her bed as I watched her, absorbed in the fullness of her silhouette, drinking in the wholeness of her presence as if it were an elixir for my exhaustion, but an ancient sickness turned my stomach.

I felt my heart race when she turned around, between the shifting of weight on the mattress and intensity of her eyes, and I almost wished that we could be something more than the incongruences that divided us.

She climbed on top of me, either seductively or sarcastically, her cleavage fully exposed by the cut of her dress, whispering nothings about being a healer. “With all this talk of fire, maybe I should share my heat with you,” she said.

The resistance in my muscles still restrained me, that feeling of sickness and a string of words I would never forget that I wanted so badly to . . .

“. . . Valentina got me this dress, Titus, you know why it’s my favorite?” she asked.

“Why’s that,” I managed.

“Because you always need someone else to take it off,” she said, her nose pressing against my cheek as I inhaled the colors of her fragrance.

I decided to act to not ruin her pleasure: “I think it’s so much better that way,” I said, an absurd feeling of guilt forcing itself to blend with my libido. I felt my hands fumble around the back of her dress, and with the last energy, I collapsed against the mattress.

She straddled me, the weight of her body pressed on my torso as I watched her rise from my chest. She arched her back as she grinded against me, peeling her dress off as she eyed me lustfully, bearing the fruit of a feeling that I couldn’t release, so all I could do was try to hold back.

I lifted her frame onto the bed as she closed her eyes in anticipation, feeling my left arm reach under her head as my right arm embraced her. My lips pressed against her neck as she playfully moaned—though I could feel the discomfort of a long-unforgotten past surface inside her, too.

We were trying: trying to fill the spaces broken between us, trying to brave the soreness of a certain pain that saturated each of us, my desires opposing her fears, and her warmth opposing my bitter coldness.

My masculine side drank in to her surrender, that surrender in her face when she gave in to my strength, the loveliness of her voice as she called for me, and I could taste the exhausting on her sweat, the sound of her heavy breathing as the only sound beyond the snow pattering against the windows, and the distant laughter of other cadets beyond the walls.

But I couldn’t distinguish between moans and grunts in the sounds between her breaths. “Is this okay?” I asked.

She opened her eyes softly. “You’re fine, keep going.”

I continued to kiss her, feeling the curvature of the fullness of her breasts, but—”Are you sure, Sylvia?”

“You’re fine, Titus,” she said. “You know I trust you.”

So as my right hand braced her shoulder, I let my left hand act on its own: she found it nestled behind her neck, my thumb caressing the side of her face as her lips pressed against mine, her teeth softly clasped around my bottom lip though I felt them chatter, as her body squirmed against me. My hand traced against the curvature of her frame until my fingers reached the fabric of her robe, causing her to flinch in protest, bashing her head against mine as I retreated.

“I—I’m sorry, I’m just not ready for that, again,” she said, “you know what . . .”

“It’s alright, I should have known,” I said. “I’m not going to force you to do anything.”

I cursed myself for the relief I felt in our withdrawal. I hated that it all felt like an act. I was so drawn to her, and maybe I loved her, but the act of sharing those feelings felt like squeezing the ocean through the eye of a needle, of a gap that was still too tight.

Her fingers traced against my core, apologetically, as I wrapped my hands higher: her fingers found their way through my dense forest of hair. The coldness of my flesh contrasted the warmth of her body as she wrapped the thickness of her legs around me, like the vines that clung to the Academy suites in the summer.

But I wouldn’t relax. And I hated myself. And my muscles were tense. And I wouldn’t give in to her.

“It’s okay to rest,” she said. “Let me take care of you.”

“It’s alright, I can keep going,” I said, terrified, heart pounding in my chest—I wanted it so badly, but I couldn’t bring myself to ask—I felt my father speaking for me.

“You don’t have to protect me all the time,” she whispered, “just relax. Open up to me.”

Open up to me.

And then I remembered. All of it.

The smell of his breath. I could feel the tension in her legs like the hands that grasped my neck, the darkness of the room lit by a single window—the feeling of heat in my ears as if frostbite had consumed them, that feeling I felt of redness in my eyes, the poisonous scream of my lungs when I tried to shout, but there was no fuel for me to breathe—

“Open up to me, Titus!” Moira said. “You have to tell me what your father did,” she said, the blood trickling from the crown of her skull, trying to remember though it was all so fast and unbelievable, so impossible in the moment to process all those emotions like flooding a reservoir that would take lifetimes to drain through a single crack. And I looked up at my mother when I laid in her lap—her nails tracing through my hair with that look of horror—as if she could feel the approach of death before it came to her—and that bastard in the corner who wouldn’t forgive himself for the rest of his life—not until death came to take his shift at the store, and I realized I was breathing way too fast as Sylvia’s loving embrace tightened, but there wasn’t any glass shattered on the floor, no shouting from downstairs, so I let my lungs fill with her breath as if it were an elixir that could free me from that pain for a while.

And then the sickness returned.

A part of me wanted to shatter entirely, and I envisioned it: Do you know how much it hurt me when she died? The pain stung behind my eyes without tears. Ellie’s been so strong, I’m just so proud of her, I wanted to say, but I just couldn’t surrender to her, I just couldn’t give in to—

I felt the stabbing shivers down my spine as she slid her nails against my skin. I couldn’t bring myself to believe her heart beat for me, in this moment, and so I tore myself from her, running, running, just like that night when the crimson fragments of glass shattered across the floor, the blood I saw when I descended the stairs, the intensity of her fragrance like the alcohol on my father’s breath, and her grasp draining my fullness of breath.

Gods, it felt so nice to feel the touch of her, but I was running, just like the last time I ever really cried before it was suffocated out of me.

And then she spoke.

“I want you to know that I’m always watching you, ever since the orphanage, ever since you saved me from it, ever since you showed me that kindness, because . . .”

I braced to deflect the brunt of her words as if they could shatter me, and I left her before she could finish her sentence.

I had my apartment for almost a year, and it was the third time I had visited it.

Ellie didn’t want me. Clint was fighting on his own. And the trifold pamphlet from the start of the year still sat on the counter, reading CONGRATULATIONS CADET, ENJOY YOUR STAY AT THE ACADEMY LOFTS.

My suitcases still sat against the wall, collecting dust, filled with a whole lot of clothes I hadn’t even worn yet. The floors were pristine: the sheetless standard-issue bed without a comforter, either.

I laid on the hardwood floor. Perverse thoughts in my mind told me I didn’t deserve the comfort of the bed, so I laid there, listening to the reverberations of a party below me. A whole lot of engineering work sat on the desk: thermal system design, kinetic properties of essence, and some other general elective I hadn’t been to all quarter.

I let time pass by for what felt like hours, until I thought my guilt would lift me from the floor and bring me back to her room to apologize, but it never came. I was bored. And I was bored of being bored, but nothing sounded interesting.

I thought about the radiant glow of the necklace enveloping my father’s shop—it wasn’t the first time that an outwaller artifact had gone off on us. In fact, it was my only proof that a god might exist, and the absurdity of those moments often blended into the dullness of my daily life, saturated in propaganda posters, in crimson-font books administered by the Ministry of Courage, and in the repetition of combat training where we were pitted against other fireteams.

The hardwood floor didn’t bother me. I was used to sleeping on rough surfaces after years of survival training in the Academy, training as my team’s Tactician, my team’s unofficial leader, and so I felt myself melt into the hardness of the floor, the dreams of water quickly approaching, accepting the lack of oxygen with a certain peace as that distant glow radiated, the waves on the surface lapping in bright orange, a distant scream—

A sound at the corner of the stairwell awoke me. Heavy footfalls cascaded against the carpet, then I turned my eyes to the clock on the wall. It was almost the afternoon of the next day. The footsteps were heavy, but not that heavy—was it Valentina, maybe? Or Sylvia? No way she’d come down here. And wait, no way it could be for me—

A fist pounded on the door. “Titus, Titus, open up,” a voice slurred.

The voice sounded drunk, male. But it wasn’t Romin.

“Who’s asking?”

“Dammit, open the door, son.”

“Clint? What are you doing here?”

“Just open up,” he demanded.

My deadbeat, drunken dad stayed in the store as if he was chained to it. Why was he here? I heard his heavy breathing—either he took a flight of stairs too fast, or was on the verge of throwing up.

I lifted myself from the floor, cracking the door open.

But I was wrong. He wasn’t drunk.

“Sons’a bitches broke in, Titus,” he said, “I couldn’t stop them, they took that damn thing you brought in. Told them we were closed, told them we’d come in the morning, bastards wearin’ all black, and…”

“Slow down,” I said. His face swelled with bruises. Dried blood ran in two parallel lines from his nose, and he could hardly speak. “When did this happen?”

“I was sleepin’ in the back room. Heard a knock on the door, told them the store was closed,” he said. “Said they’d be willing to pay handsomely for that thing you brought in earlier, knew we had it. I told them to wait ‘till sunup when we were open, but ‘no,’ they said, ‘they’ll kill you for it if we don’t take it now,’ they said.”

“And what were they wearing?” I asked.

“All black,” he said. “Said it was for the good of the city, but they didn’t look like guards. Said they were trying to keep all the humans alive. One last refusal then they kicked down the door, a whole lot of ‘em, took the necklace right out of the container.”

I asked, “So what does that have to do with me?”

“You mean, your injured father? Broken doors that’ll cost a week’s earnings for a locksmith? It just so happens that the amulet wasn’t the only thing in there, son,” Clint said, “and Grandpa didn’t always write in scribbles for his notes.”

He passed the letter to me, inviting himself in to sit on my desk. The vellum was still saturated in his blood from the night he cut his finger. “Dear Lyra,” it read:

The Amulet within this box is the artifact that has passed through generations, fought over by three different blood lines of Humans. The transgressions of a decade will never lighten their load on my heart, and I hope you understand that this box was what I had to protect. Within it is the future of the Humans: your chance at becoming the Chosen, the chance for you to follow the purest bloodline by birthright in your brother’s passing. I mourn his sacrifice in protecting you. But the future of our people lies in the strength of our connection with the Gods, by connecting with your Guardian of divine blood, and just like your father, and just like your father’s mother. You are the last blood tie that holds the Carmine blood line to the amulet, the purest bloodline passed through generations.

The emergence of this artifact signifies the birth of a new golden era: of equity, of peace, of progress, and the abolishment of those atheistic views leveraged by the affluent, trying to take their own people away from the Gods that watch over them. I pray not for your forgiveness but for understanding. Realize the gravity of this situation before hatred decides for you. I’ve preached the details since you were a child. The knowledge of the Path resides within your mind; it cannot be taken by anyone.

“This Lyra,” I said. “Who was she to Everett?”

“I’m not sure,” Clint said. “there’s a whole side of our family that secluded themselves. Your grandfather Everett had a brother—I heard of him when I was younger though I never met him. The name was tossed around by Everett in passing.”

“A brother? Why did you never tell me?”

“Because I never met him, because he never talked with us. When I was in Seminary, a little after Ellie’s age, Everett would leave me in the care of your Aunt Elen. My sister. And every quarter of the year, he’d disappear for days at a time: staying in contact with this brother, trying to convince him of the value of his blood or somethin’ but all I knew was every time, he’d come home dejected.”

“For days at a time,” I said. “He must have lived on the other side of the city.”

“I thought the same thing at first,” he said. “Until I saw him packing heavy. He’d take his sword with him. He’d dress for intense weather. And most of all, your grandfather was a Frostpunk, just like us.”

“So he went beyond the walls, then,” I said, “but who would choose to live beyond the walls with the Chymaera? Exposed to danger, without food or shelter.”

“People don’t choose to live beyond the walls, son,” Clint said, “you should know that from how desperate those Chymaerans are— or those ‘good’ ones the Merlot keep bitching about.”

“So you’re saying they were out there because they were forced to,” I said.

“It’s a guess,” Clint said. “And Everett made me swear I never spoke about it.”

“Huh,” I replied.

He said, “But one day, around twenty, my father had disappeared again. My sister tried for the Academy—she made it into the lowest class, so she was transferred into city work, unable to travel beyond the walls. I was still in the Academy, Dragon class, just like you, all bright-eyed and excited to get out. That day, I was watching the house since my sister wasn’t there. The city guards came by, saying that Everett had sent them: it wasn’t unusual, since his storehouse was a common trade outlet he leased to government-run trade companies, so I let them in.”

“The Berguard Trade Company. The building you were planning on selling.”

He reclined against the wall as he gasped to catch a breath, staring across the room through the single eye that could find me. “The building I’ve closed on. It’s being sold to the Carmine.”

“Wait—are you kidding me? And you didn’t even ask me first,” I said, “how dare you—”

“Let me finish, son,” Clint said, “do you wanna hear the story or not?”

I huffed in protest.

He pointed towards the map spread across the table. “So they found the map in Everett’s study. Documents. And in that moment, I knew they weren’t here for trade, but it was too late,” he said, “and so they took the map. That time he returned from beyond the walls, something changed inside of him, something deep inside of him. Something snapped that was irreversible, something that forced him to condemn himself, so he spent the rest of his days pursuing something that he lost.”

“But Everett was so different with me,” I said.

“That’s because you were young. He figured I was old enough to fend for myself,” Clint said, “and I was. Soon after I had a wife and a kid. And he had his mission.”

“And traveling beyond the walls was how he got the Chymaeran Curse.”

“Around the time that your sister was born. We tried for a child so many times, but we gave up after your mother was infertile, you know that,” he said, “that’s why we got you.”

“You ‘got’ me. What, like something you’d buy from the store?” I said.

“We were blessed with you, I mean,” he said, “blessed to adopt you from the orphanage, after Moira fell in love with you. And I was impressed by your fighting—you were top of all the kids there . . . and I liked you too, of course. Son.”

“So what does this have to do with Lyra?”

Clint said, “She’s the daughter of that brother. And I don’t know who she is, I don’t know where she comes from, and I don’t know where she lives, but that’s the only news I’ve ever heard. Ever since, he’s only rambled about the Chosen, all the fiction of the worlds beyond the walls—though nobody’s lived long enough to survive a week out there. Why else do we send you out off the academy saying it’s a celebration, Titus,” he exclaimed, “when you know it’s a mass funeral!”
