“Why do you wear that stupid scarf when you live so close to the Compass Rose? It’s spring here, all the time,” I said, tugging on her crimson-threaded fabric.

Valentina Vermillion protested in the seat across from me. “It’s just special to me, Titus. It’d take too long to explain,” she muttered, sardonically scanning the onlookers. Their glances shimmered with recognition when they noticed her, and the embers in their eyes spread it like firebrands from a brush fire: soon everybody was watching us, disbelieving their favorite heroine from the Academy network programming rode before them. The streets were ragged: unpaved to code for such a fine vehicle, and the bumps shook it in shivering tremors like winters for the homeless. They looked on as if they had never seen something so beautiful, the way the rosy vermilion color saturated the chassis like a savory sour-drop: surrounded by a sea of green, brown, and extra brown, the only colors the Outwallers could afford.

“Just a little bit further,” I told the driver. The car coughed smoke. The further we left the Academy the further the weather dropped, but the cold was nostalgic. There was the path I took every day to the orphanage to see her—the only walk my mother allowed me “beyond the mailbox where I can see you,” since the slanted crack of every sidewalk branded my mind like charcoal scratchings on texture.

She was standing in the doorway, all six-feet of her hunched to almost half the height, her mopped mess of hair always damp from a one-oh-clock-bath-or-shower parted to the side with a single strand of hair hanging between her eyes, miles different from the girl who sat next to me, whose radiant, corn-silk hair carried such a brightness you could almost say it was effervescent, and I could smell its presence when she raised her arm to wave.

Sylvia muttered a faint greeting.

Valentina replied, “Hey yourself, girl, are you ready to go?”

“This car’s so bright if the sun caught it wrong it would catch fire,” Sylvia said, and paused for a moment after she placed her first foot—choosing my side as if the fate of our friendship hung in the balance, the seat wide enough for three, but she pressed against me.

“It’s nice you’re with us,” Valentina said. Instead of all by yourself in your room, she inferred.

“And it’s nice to have an excuse to get out of the winter,” Sylvia said, “it’s hard to remember the last time I saw you outside the Academy, or even the last time our schedules lined up like this.”

The car churned up a cough and sputtered into motion: the inertia of Sylvia’s body leaned against me with double the force.

“You probably see Titus all the time since you live a floor apart, but I only see you at training,” Valentina said, “and every other time, I see you in scrubs. So that surgeon-thing must be going well.”

“Taking out the broken parts and placing back the good, who knew it could ever be so hard?” Sylvia laughed. “But it’s great, finished my training for combat medic.”

“Pray tell, darling, I could never touch blood like that,” Valentina said.

“Combat-medic-archer,” I said. “So what, do you shoot medical supplies at us when we get hurt?”

Sylvia pinched me.

“Ouch,” I said. Then I turned to Valentina to finish the roast.

“Being afraid of blood isn’t exactly an option when you’re going out to combat like the rest of us.”

“It’s just that I don’t prefer it,” Valentina said.

I laughed. “What does that mean? How can you ’prefer’ something else?”

Sylvia said, “It’s so cold beyond the walls I’m sure their blood will freeze before it touches the ground, anyways.”

I repeated, “Then what do you prefer?”

Valentina blushed. “I work with knives like Sylvia. I just don’t see the clean-up,” she said, turning her glance beyond the window with a repulsion like magnets, probably just waiting until the moment that Romin could back her up, so I lightened up on her.

“You definitely have the brilliance for Reconnaissance, at least,” I said, so I could relish in her response. She turned her head even further until I mistook her for an owl.

“Don’t take me down to your level, then try and hit on me,” she said. “You plebeians wish you could be so rich and influential like me: pray tell, it must be so, so hard. This life is literally a dream.”

“Really?” Sylvia and I said.

She laughed. “Not at all.”

“What’s not?” a booming voice called: like a crack of thunder before his face flashed beyond the chassis. It was a first to be looking down on him.

Romin tried a step to get into the vehicle, and it felt as if we were a ship sinking stern-down, and I was glad he sat on Valentina’s side: he had a whole extra head on her and made her family’s Model-F look no larger than a toy truck.

And then it was my second time I looked down on him: the chassis shifted in italics until his muscular frame bent closer to the center, and with everyone out of words, Romin opened his fat mouth to cut the silence, asking the question that was on everyone’s minds:

“So where are we off to?”

“Tell the driver to take a left here,” I said, “even though he probably won’t want to. I know he knows the kind of people that live here, and it’s a whole different class from what you’re you’re used to, Valentina.”

“My people!” Romin said. He pointed out every curved crimson symbol etched in the doorways. “My Brothers and Sisters of the Carmine Syndicate stretch far beyond the foothills of the Compass Rose.”

The air was cold. None of the geothermal energy from the dormant volcano graced the Outwallers: the infertile, the impoverished, whose genes and lineage never brought them the fortune of a child they could train for the Academy. My luck lies in the line of my grandfather, Everett, who fought beyond the walls long before the Carmine and the king twisted the city of Blackwater into their capital city, before they installed the livid hatred of the Chymaera that’s kept our city safe for almost a hundred years. Our name reaches further in my father’s service as a Cadet. And one day, I hoped my sister would follow in my footsteps.

Just by reading the temperature of their eyes I could tell their experience with Blackwater’s far lands: the smoke saturated the sky, holding back sunshine and starlight. This city’s land wasn’t meant for life of any kind, at least beyond the warmth of hot springs and the passion of bright Cadets. Romin kept rambling about the “promise” of the Far Lands for the Carmine, and how important the powers of our essence are, how we need to preserve and not repress them like all the “rich people” and the “man” are trying to take away.

Valentina trained her glance on the floor, the only surface left that held any coat of polish, stewing a rebuttal about how essence is a waste, how persecution for using the powers of life and fire are a waste. Romin said “using those powers keeps them warm,” asking her if “she’s forgotten what temperature is living in that big house of hers,” sacrificing any chance at sex for the night over the conviction of his argument. Sylvia’s crimson eyes weighed shut from the bags under her eyes, and anytime they opened, they seemed to steal a glance at me: and I found myself returning them, too, until she rested against my shoulder like a ragdoll and I put my arm around her to keep her upright.

The smoke from the generators was so thick I couldn’t even see the ten-mile-diameter of the Compass Rose: the houses sunk in the blizzard like tiny blocks in a basin of flour until they merged with the horizon, and as I coughed and covered my mouth, I began to envy Valentina’s vermilion scarf.

A similar signpost signified our approach. “Here it is,” I said.

“Here? What’s ‘here’?” Valentina said, still refusing her surroundings over the polished floor.

“Our passage out,” I said, “ask your cabbie what time it is.”

She scoffed at the word ‘cabbie,’ then asked: “Charles, what time is it?”

Fuckin’ Charles, I thought: Romin sniggered over the name, too.

“Half past three, Miss Vermillion,” Fuckin’ Charles said.

“’Half past three,’ he said,” Valentina replied.

“I know, my unprivileged, plebeian ears can hear him,” I said, “that means it’s just about time.”

“Time for what?” Romin asked. “Is this another egress point on the Railroad?”

“It’s a new one. I doubt even most of your traders know about it,” I said. “Because I’m the one that found it.”

“Egress point?” Valentina asked.

“A point for Frostpunks to get beyond the walls, to find things to trade,” I said, “to go off without being detected. The city is big. But they often patch the holes in the walls: the government puts moles in with the traders. But luckily it’s just my father, my sister and I, and a single shop he keeps alive as the last image of my mother.”

Our boots touched the ground after Romin’s boats touched down first (I could almost hear the hydraulic sigh of relief from the Model-F). The alley was unmarked by any traders’ symbols: it was still fresh and untouched by frostpunks’ hands. The distant vibrations of the train shook the ground beneath us, a giant earthworm that would soon break the surface of the tunnel beneath us.

Sylvia’s speech slurred with prepositions: “Down there? Over the rail? On the side…” she stammered, but the tremors grew stronger. And then a shrill whistle broke the air: its pitch sinking like a slide whistle as the thunder boomed louder.

“The trains slow down aboveground,” I said, “so aim for an open car.” Valentina nodded: with her stealth training she was like a cat. Without hesitation in her spring she landed on her feet while the rest of us crumpled, and I almost laughed when Romin put a dent in the bed of the car. The sudden change in inertia flung us against the back, where we found a window to a closed-car.

I said, “This will get us past inspection. They always do a half-ass job.” If twelve-sterling-an-hour was a velocity, or if wages could be defined by speed, it’d be slower than molasses.

“As long as you know where you’re going,” Sylvia said.

“It’s alright,” I said, “Everett’s map has never been wrong before about the outer lands.” He spent the whole end of his life searching for something. The page saturated with scribbles: unbelievable to think that every etched line was an individual thought. Documents about the outer lands were forbidden (since a tightly-enforced evening curfew prevented egress of the inexperienced)—and Everett’s documents were the life-blood of our little shop.

“Let’s just hope the Chymaera don’t catch us,” I said.

“The Chymaeran extremists,” said Valentina.

“A name that covers the lot of them,” Romin said. “And if you understood what they do, you’d think different.”

“Differently,” Valentina said. “And remember, before the Carmine refugees fled here, Merlot like us lived alongside them.”

“The Chymaeran non-extremists,” said Romin.

“The name that actually covers the lot of them,” Valentina said. “My family deals in the, well… ‘business’ side of Blackwater, too. The Vermillion Estate grants the most work visas out of any organization.”

“And probably the reason why those face-stealers kill our kind and steal their identities, just to survive,” Romin said.

“Whoa, those are all myths, Romin,” Sylvia cut in. “Myths. Entirely refutable and untrue.”

“Then why did the Academy’s Carmine cadets catch one the other day? You heard the report.” Break this up a little more with some motion and movement. It feels like a bit of an exposition dump.

“And you probably didn’t read the They agreed upon the transference in private so that the Chymaeran could survive, not forced to live beyond the walls like some kind of savage.”

“But that is savage,” I said. “Eating someone’s flesh to take their identity is ridiculous and barbaric,” I managed, before Sylvia’s crimson glare shot me down.

“And forcing them to live beyond the walls when they lived here before the Carmine is ridiculous—just because the Carmine are refugees that once bordered Chymaeran extremists doesn’t mean they’re all bad,” Valentina said.

“Not when you use them for a certain . . . business, though,” Romin replied.

“Gross,” Valentina said, “you’re not implying—”

“It’s old news that people save the ashes of old lovers. And the real perverse can steal something from the people they dream about, and—”

“Enough!” Sylvia said. “We’re not talking about this.”

“Exactly, because the Merlot don’t do that,” Valentina said.

Guess Romin’s out of sex for a month now, I thought to myself—any further and they might hate-fuck or something. It was a sheer miracle they mixed together, like water and oil or orange juice and milk. The more I watched them, the more I convinced myself their relationship was all just a political move between the two largest families, as some kind of olive-branch offering or contingent reason to never fight (but at least being in a fireteam with them meant life was never uneventful.)

“So how long until we get there?” Romin asked, and how long do I have to sit next to her? his voice implied.

You’re the one in the relationship. “We’ll pass the walls in several hours,” I replied—I’d typically just sleep it off, but everyone else brought something to pass the time: Sylvia worked on one of her commissioned pro- Academy posters. Valentina rehearsed lines for the next episode with a musical intonation in her voice. And Romin clinked through the bottles in his bag: his hobby was being an alcoholic since the effects never seemed to reach him, and it helped him forget about his brother. And maybe it helped him forget about his brother. I don’t know.

And I decided to sleep.

It wasn’t the siren of curfew that awoke me— I was fortunate the train passed the walls long before it, but a certain heavenly warmth greeted me instead.

Sylvia sprawled against the slats of the train car: her hair’s entanglement in the splinters suspended her like a marionette from invisible strings. Her finished piece in the corner made me wonder how much time had passed. Romin’s snore was loud, but only got louder with every empty handle—his throat’s static matched their clank with every jump of the train car. It was hard to believe that Valentina had fallen asleep, too (and a quandary how it’s possible to fall asleep photogenically in a train car,) hugging the script to her chest like it was her little sister.

If there’s one thing I can’t complain about the Academy, the outfits are definitely one of them. Men’s upper-class cloaks are aesthetic (aesthetic in this usage is kind of a new-age slang, so I’d do something different about): at the highest class, cadets get ruby shoulder-pads, a glimmering crimson “mark” (or cape that’s shorter and far more manageable), and combat pads infused with essence. But Romin and I agree we appreciate the cut of the women’s warm-weather gear a lot better.

She must have lifted my head to keep me from the floor—the tightness of her long-socks kept her legs firm with fresh, warm blood, and looking up, I could only catch a hint of her face from the cut of her cleavage. And it all sounds erotic, but I couldn’t feel anything—the more I see her beauty, the sadder it makes me feel for her, and the more it brings me back to her fall. She was one of the most beautiful people I had seen: the way she blossomed after our shared childhood. Bringing her and Valentina around was the trick Romin and I used for success at parties. But the chase lost its heat after the incident . . . I told her not to drink, and I still blame myself for not being around. She started covering her body. She started eating more, then eating nothing, then eating more, than eating nothing, and started rolling down the sleeves on her wrists and wearing watches and bracelets.

I turned my head to the side and traced the flesh of her legs, the first low-effort trial-line I noticed that sparked our first argument. Light rolls constrict in her outfit, and sometimes I wonder if she debates between being called fat or facing the fear of being taken advantage of again. Her head dips against her shoulder. A tear floats across her face: I can hear the echoed nightmares of a distant past I saved her from before all this. BLOOD EYES! CRIMSON DEMON—but I’m not surprised kids in the orphanage could be so mean, they just didn’t learn how adults could “maturely” turn suffering inward instead of unleashing it on others. But then again, I guess Sylvia just blossomed early.

I reached for her face to wipe the streak although my own sickness caught me—or namely, maybe just a certain arrangement of words and habits from my own past. I felt myself roll away from her warmth and sprawl across the splinters of the cabin again, my head tilted towards the sky, matching Romin’s drunken sprawl but not his snore. Strands of moonlight poured through the slats of the ceiling, tangling themselves tightly in luminous lines: the locomotive’s clouded columns scratched the strands like a bow with the thinnest frequencies of tinnitus, reminiscent of the sound of Ellie’s strings.

The smell of ozone glazed my attention—the Academy always warned it was the sign of danger—and the trees glowed amethyst beyond the slats—brighter and brighter as our destination approached.

“So we’ll catch the twelve-o’-clock back,” Romin said, “I managed to grab the shipping records from our warehouse. Arrives in Blackwater at three-thirty, so we have a quarter-day to explore—So show us what you do, Titus.”

“I will,” I said. Romin might graze the clichés of numb-skull brute, but he was damn good with numbers. After all, he was slated to take over the full family business—whose influence employed a near quarter of Blackwater (and you could tell their employees by the bright crimson paraphernalia they so proudly wore.)

Valentina still had snow still stuffed in her hat from the jump, but she refused to remove it, so instead, she checked the weapons stealthily installed across her body. We looked like dough-rollers fresh out of the kitchen, dusted by sheets of flour.

Sylvia shook the ice crystals from her eyelashes before she examined the map. The bow wrapped around her body like a shoulder-bag at the opposite angle of her quiver. “Due north and east,” she shouted.”

“The train snakes along the ridge until it reaches the cliff. The line right here.” My finger traced the map. “But I’ve never explored this ‘X’ before—the way it’s been referenced and circled in his journals means it’ll be a big find, but I never had the time to get this far.”

“Well, it’s nice to help the poor every so often,” Valentina said.

“I’m just thankful for your patronage, ma’am,” I said, returning her a bow. “Give me papers later and I’ll sign off on your privilege license.”

“Fair enough, Titus, let’s get this over with, then,” she replied. “If I’m not back on time I’ll miss the money-burning party, then I won’t get to eat caviar and complain about the warm weather.” (Have Titus say this to her. A cheeky back and forth)

“Do you really do that?” Romin asked.

“Oh, of course,” she said. “Poor Carmine, it must be so lonely and cold down the hill.”

“Then you won’t even fathom how it feels for me,” I said.

“Well, it’s not my fault you don’t sleep at your Academy loft,” Valentina said. “But I’ll give one thing to you, though—that little sister of yours is so cute I can’t blame you for taking care of her so much.”

“She deserves a better childhood than I had. . . ” a certain tenderness poured out of my voice before I cut it— “ . . . she deserves a good childhood, you know?”

Sylvia nods in recognition—maybe she stirs through several memories. The train’s shrill whistle is low-pitched and far beyond us. All that was left was sheets of virgin snow. The lack of footprints set us all in an uncomfortable ease, as if the smell of ozone would sift the air when we least anticipated it.

Romin’s hands halted at his sides. Two revolvers rested in their holsters, waiting for a snap of deliverance, which made my sword and Valentina’s knives seem unnecessary, but the Academy instructors always insisted they were right: the first to show battle scars and trade war stories. They’ll attack you before you know it, before you can even pull the trigger or hear their clicks or their glowing eyes, the instructors said, and you should pray you never see what they look like, those bastards, I had to kill one after he stole my fireteam-mate’s identity, and you should pray you never see them halfway through transforming, like melted flesh, like something dead-and-alive that’s half-human and half-not, and it’s… incredibly effective at scaring the young Cadets. That’s why they paid Sylvia to paint posters. I guess then I was in the transition phase, or the second stage of fear like a second puberty—right before graduation when all the uncertainty and fears came back, all the “oh Gods, what’s it really like beyond the walls?”

They didn’t show us for a reason. The ambiguous and imagined will always be far more frightening than the proven. It was like Sylvia the first week after starting medical school, when she got the “Human Book Of Known Diseases” and thumbed through it in a night like one of the government’s outlawed books about the Chosen legends—when she started believing she had all those diseases, and all I could do was laugh at her.

The walk was quiet since quiet was so rare. And also from the crack of every branch. Romin sweat bullets as if he could use them as live ammunition, so he turned his big, bald, black head from his girlfriend. Valentina played stoic as if she were a renowned television celebrity, and Sylvia stayed close to me—I could see them all running in their minds from the concept of nighttime.

If worst comes to worst, we could just stay out here in a house, I could say. Then they’d all just nervously nod their heads (or Valentina would be the first to sprint and cry for the next train), but as amusing as that would be, I decided not to bring it up.

The air grew colder with a bitter smell. They probably thought it was ozone.

“Just geothermal activity in the area,” I said, “but we’re fine, we’re almost there.” While they were busy being nervous, I thumbed through Everett’s old records, mostly flooded with indiscernible scribbles and disjointed words—a single crest consistent across all of them with our family name: “Berguard.” I was young when he approached the end of his life, when the ability to sleep was stolen from him by the Chymaeran Curse. His hands trembled. His writing accelerated. He mumbled and cried about something he had forgotten, and eventually he got so scary my father Clint refused for me to see him: then eventually my stoic father was too terrified to see him.

He died in his sleep, they said, but I know that was a lie. He stayed awake for a full month before he died—the autopsy says his brain dissolved to a tenth of its size. How could you tell it was the curse? I asked. Because gray matter doesn’t glow purple, they said.

But it’s only the specialized Chymaerans, the Cadets say, only, like, a percent of a percent can do that, but nobody’s proven that, either. If there was some magic hundred-chamber revolver and I loaded it with a single bullet, you wouldn’t let me press it against your head and pull the trigger—neither would Romin, Sylvia, or Valentina—and would you even trust I loaded just one bullet? Confirmation bias and experience is the only reason I didn’t tremble—on the first trips before my mother Moira died, Clint told me he’d protect me no matter what, and I felt so safe.

But the night of my mother’s death is the reason I started protecting myself, and the reason why I’ll never forgive him—if two broken pieces could always match, then maybe Sylvia and I were perfect for each other—but damaged goods didn’t fracture the same. I always felt we were patching spaces where our imperfections failed to meet: but in time I’d learn we just failed to muster the courage to patch the space between us.

The door took a full shoulder before it gave in. The hinges screeched from beyond a century of snow-borne rust as chips of pine wood tore from the bottom, leaving the door with jagged, gnashing teeth. It siphoned outside air as if the house had lungs to breathe.

“Hello?” I called, just to break the silence and see if I hadn’t forgotten how to speak. The house’s siding groaned in response, breathing winds into its lungs, and a single chandelier hung from the wooden cross-beam like a uvula. Dormant lanterns circled the chains as crystalline fragments shimmered—Valentina was probably glancing at them with some reminiscence of home. There were no voices outside the walls like our apartments at the Academy, footsteps from upper floors, but even in the silence, we still felt like we were being watched.

It felt as if some agent was consuming all the light in the house, opposite of the way that light bulbs and lanterns throw their colors. Romin dragged several matches against the floor. Three sputtered in sparks before the gravity of the scene finally weighed on us, or before our terrors could even take shape, before the manifestation of an ancient bloodbath brought itself into focus: painted across the floors as if the glass of the crimson paintings had shattered under pressure and spilled their contents.

I looked at the map, then the ground, then back to the map—in the split second my fireteam felt fear’s flame feather in their lungs, my brain sputtered.

Valentina opened her mouth to scream, how much she hated blood, but the bass in Romin’s voice beat her to it.

“Gods,” he said. But that was it: his face plain and neutral as if this scene had graced him a thousand times before. “Chymaeran bastards,” he muttered several times.

With her medical experience, Sylvia could say she owned the color red from all the cadavers she cut. Our steps clattered on the linoleum floor of the kitchen before ages-old splashes of not-ketchup flooded the floors and a sickening smell saturated the air. The bones must have been picked apart by scavengers: the meat left clean like a rib roast after a hundred starving cadets.

I threw my bag on the counter, scattering journals and notebooks and pages and artifacts—all I brought was all I inherited from my grandfather. And all across them was the same insignia, the same letter I saw etched in the doorway, the same set of strokes on the stationery that my grandfather spilled the last pieces of his mind on, the same symbol reflecting on the stone above the hearth of the fireplace, and . . .

Valentina sunk under Romin’s arm like an afterthought as Sylvia checked the corpse: illuminated by faint beams of sunlight through the tattered shades. “I’m not trained as a mortician,” she said. “But there’s deep scratches on the vertebrae—must have been a slice to the carotid artery, and they bled out on the floor.”

Romin calmly strolled across the carpet with the lantern. His fingers traced desperate scratches on the door, followed by jagged, splintered teeth around the doorknob as if someone had tried to break out: bludgeoned indentations that grew weaker and weaker. “Damn Chymaerans must have been trying to break in—the rest of the family must have been hiding in here,” he said.

“But there’s no trace of Chymaeran essence along the corpse’s scratches. When Human blood and Chymaeran essence make contact, it glows purple, then leaves a dry chalk residue,” Sylvia said.

“So it was Human, then?” said Valentina.

“That’s right,” I said, “Chymaeran attacks look a whole lot different.” I kept scanning through the documents and notebooks, all signed with the same cursive letter at the bottom.

“By Gods, this wasn’t Chymaeran at all, look at the marks,” she said. Tiny handprints. Like handprints of a desperate child: then the size of the clawed scratchings all made sense. The notes grew no more intelligible: further and further until I got to the bottom, to the map that sprawled across the table. And the necklace Sylvia found on the corpse had the same insignia: the letter B.

We had been using this map for years. All the houses, all the terrain other explorers hadn’t charted, beyond the walls, beyond the eye’s reach of the train tracks, all the creases and folds that had been left to my grandfather’s memories, and all those X’es had been circling around one location: the desperation of Everett’s brainless scratchings reaching a crescendo, as if all his cerebral emotions of some long-passed moment had bled in warning. That this map wasn’t for treasure.

“This was Everett’s house,” I said, just as Romin’s kick bludgeoned the door into a shower of splinters. The rancid odor of death only grew stronger, but besides the blood of the corpse and the scratchings on the door, upturned furniture, and shattered glass all across the carpet, there was nothing else: wildlife and nature had picked the entire scene clean.

But not the locked room, some kind of study, which held Romin and Valentina in curiosity, glowing in the dying western light as if it had been preserved so perfectly in amber. Romin asked what kind of coward would leave a child outside to die: but no corpses of children turned up in any corner of the house, no matter how hard we scanned. Our time was running short. This wasn’t a murder mystery, after all, but a Frostpunk loot run, and as the sun descended, it cast its final rays on all the reflective surfaces—metallic items, jewelry, black-font forbidden books, and the four of us loaded sleds in haste.

Silence swept over us for nearly an hour until Romin’s voice severed it, something had been bothering him. “They weren’t protecting themselves,” he called, “it just doesn’t make sense. It had to have been something else.”

“Un-shattered glass in the study, the windows still intact, all locked in the down position—you see this door locks from the inside, and it was locked.”

“So if the door was locked, why did they stay in the room?”

He began to fervently scan the study, but the sun was setting, already past the pine trees, setting the entire room in a fading afterglow. “We have to get home,” I said. “Remember the schedule?”

“Most days I work in ‘repo’ with the Carmine, Titus. Nobody hides anything from us or me,” Romin said. “Come on, this far, and we’re off with chump change?”

“Then check where the walls meet the carpet,” he said.

“There’s nothing in here,” Valentina said.

“Just look,” he said, “I’ve done this enough times to feel it.”

I laughed. “Ridiculous, we’re wasting daylight, there’s not a…”

The carpet folded backwards in the corner. Dusk caught one last reflection off the rectangular lid of an aluminum tin, then my fingers fondled for an edge until I lifted it from the hollowed crevice.

I shook it: something heavy inside emitted a certain curious energy, but that curiosity faded with the darkness, replaced with the anxiety of almost an hour behind. Romin ogled the box with the words I told you. His anticipation jumped when his huge ogre hands couldn’t tear the lock from the box, then he angrily tossed it on the sled.

“That better damn well be worth an hour,” I said, taking a final glance over my shoulder. The shattered teeth of the door siphoned snowflakes: we had abused and thrown around its innards for long enough, and all that remained was a single body, whose death held no gravity—at least in the moment.

Valentina voiced her concerns it would damage the inside—but Romin was mid-swing, and all we saw was the spray of sparks illumine the box before his hands emerged from the twisted metal. The moonless night brought no radiance beyond the fire in the train car. Scraps of aged vellum lined the box, legible aged vellum, legible aged vellum and a full word at the bottom that was indiscernible in the darkness.

Romin’s hands held two strands of sliver that swung with the weight of a pendant. It glowed the faintest crimson, and it was in that moment I should have known the significance of the artifact that sat for decades waiting to reach my hands.
